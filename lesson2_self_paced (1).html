📐 Grammar Revision

Comparatives & Superlatives

We use comparatives and superlatives to talk about change and differences. Start by exploring the examples below — then check the explanation before doing the exercises.

"At first, Sam felt more uncomfortable than he does now."

"Now he feels much more confident."

"Joining the running group was the best decision he made."

"This is the happiest he has felt in months."

🔍 Look at the examples and think…

Look at these two sentences. What is different about the adjective?

a) Sam is happy.

b) Sam is happier now than before.

👁 Show answer

In (b) we're comparing two situations — we added -er to the adjective. This is a comparative.

📐 Comparatives — comparing two things

We use comparatives to say that one thing is more/less of something than another.

Adjective type Rule Example
Short (1 syllable) add -er fast → faster
Ends in -y -y → -ier happy → happier
Long (2+ syllables) more + adjective comfortable → more comfortable
Irregular special forms good → better / bad → worse

💡 We always use than when we name both things being compared: "He is happier than before."

🔍 Now think about this…

Look at these two sentences. What is different?

a) Joining the group was a good decision.

b) Joining the group was the best decision he made.

👁 Show answer

In (b) we're saying this is number one — the most of everything. We use the before a superlative. This is comparing one thing to all others in its group.

🏆 Superlatives — the most extreme

We use superlatives to say something is the most/least of all in a group. Always use the before a superlative.

Adjective type Rule Example
Short (1 syllable) add -est fast → the fastest
Ends in -y -y → -iest happy → the happiest
Long (2+ syllables) the most + adjective comfortable → the most comfortable
Irregular special forms good → the best / bad → the worst
✅ Quick check — can you decide?

Which form is correct? Think before you click to reveal.

1. Living in Valencia is _______ (easy) than living in London.

👁 Show answer

easier — short adjective ending in -y → -ier. We're comparing two cities.

2. That was _______ (good) decision he ever made.

👁 Show answer

the best — irregular superlative. "ever" signals we're comparing one thing to all others.

3. She is _______ (comfortable) now than when she first arrived.

👁 Show answer

more comfortable — long adjective (3 syllables) → more + adjective. We're comparing two moments.

🧠 Grammar Focus

Present Perfect

Look at these examples from Sam's story. What do they have in common?

Sam has made several friends.
He has found a language class.
Things have changed a lot since he arrived.
He hasn't felt this comfortable in months.
💡 Think about it
All these sentences use have/has + past participle. They connect a past action or experience to the present moment — the result is still relevant NOW. We don't say exactly when it happened.

Structure

✅ Positive: Subject + have/has + past participle
❌ Negative: Subject + have/has not (haven't/hasn't) + past participle
❓ Question: Have/Has + subject + past participle…?

Common time expressions

since for already yet just ever never recently

📄 Audio Script — The Psychology of Belonging

Belonging is not just about being physically present — it is about feeling that you matter, that your absence would be noticed. Psychologists argue belonging is a fundamental need. People who feel it perform better at work, learn faster, and report higher wellbeing. Chronic loneliness has been linked to serious health consequences. The good news: belonging is something we can build. Small, consistent acts — showing up, listening, remembering details — add up over time. It has grown gradually, one small connection at a time.
